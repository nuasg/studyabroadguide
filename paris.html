IFSA Butler

Level of Difficulty - Pass/Fail
  • No need to worry too much about grades given that you pass
  • Classes were interesting, but depending on the class
    • workloads may vary, ranging from group projects to research and papers.
  • May be tough without a decent language background in Spanish

    • Receiving NU Credit
  • You can receive up to 5 credits
  • Credit earned by past students: Spanish major credits, History major credits, Ethics & Fine Arts Distro credits, SESP concentration credits

    • Must be enrolled in 15 credits
  • UCA and Usal - 3 credits per class (50 minutes thrice a week)
  • Di Tela - 5 credits per class (50 minutes thrice a week)
  • UBA - 6 credits per class (4 hours once a week)
    • Classes Taken by Previous Students
  • Argentine History (UCA)
  • Spanish class with a Cinema Focus (IFSA)
  • Intro to Domestic Violence
  • New Cinema in Latin America
  • Argentine Identity and Culture
  • Architecture and Identity in Latin America
  • Recording Buenos Aires

    • Other Requirements
  • In general, student is allowed to take classes from four different universities - UBA, UCA, Di Tela, and Usal. Schools aren't too spread apart, and most students took classes from about two different universities
  • You must take one Spanish language class
  • Everyone gets assigned to host families, mostly in one of two towns
  • Spoke Spanish with the host family most of the time
  • Families expected to provide breakfast and dinner everyday except Sundays
  • Program is accommodating and helps you switch families if you are unsatisfied with host family
  • In theory - not allowed to go to other people's host houses
  • Most friends met through the IFSA orientation (Similar to Wildcat Welcome, as you're put into groups partaking in a various activities), which lasts for 2 weeks.

  • There is a large foreign exchange program at UCA, and a lot of student groups (advertised through social media) would host events for non-Argentine students (Americans and beyond).
  • Exposed to a diverse community of peers, ranging from Northwestern students and Argentine nationals, as well as foreign exchange students from various nations.

    • Eating

    Tipping not formal, but typically 10%

    • Typically would spend about 50 pesos ($6) for each meal, multiplied by the number of times you would eat out without your host family.
    • You can buy groceries and cook your own meals
    • As families provide breakfast and dinner everyday except Sundays, you are on your own for lunch
    • Food is fairly cheap
    • A lot of pizzerias and empanada places (which you can get for typically 80 cents)
    • Drinking water isn't free, but you can ask for tapwater (usually lukewarm from the sink). Bottled water typically costs the same as soda.
    • Recommendations to try:
      • Empanadas
      • Ice-cream
      • Beef
      • Alfajores

    Time of meal

    Breakfast: Up to you
    Lunch: 12 - 2pm
    Dinner: 9pm - 12am

    Night Life

    Specific Nightclubs

  • Bars: Sugar, Cronico
  • Clubs: Kika, Terrazas, Crowbar, Asia Decua, Bolices
  • Neighborhoods: Parelmo (most visited), Plaza Serrano, Recoleta (more expensive)

    • Drinks to try
  • Quilmes (beer)
  • Fernet (Originally Italian liquor that tastes like licorice and is usually consumed with Coke)

    • What to wear
  • Girls: Typically whatever you want. Dresses, skirts, etc.
  • Guys: Typically need to dress better. NO SNEAKERS.

    • Safety
  • Never walk home alone, especially if you're drunk (bad combination)
  • No violent crime, but a lot of petty thefts (especially iPhones).
  • Stay away from La Boca, and other places you aren't familiar of

    • Typical night

    11pm/Midnight: Go to a bar
    2/3am: Head to a nightclub
    4/5am: Get home (weekdays)

    Travelling

    Travelling within Buenos Aires
  • La Feria de San Telmo (markets open on Saturday)
  • Las Madres de la Plaza de Mayo
  • El Centro
  • Recoleta Cemetery
  • Jazz Clubs (e.g. Theloneus)
  • Milongas (tango-houses, as Argentina is the birthplace of tango)
  • Bomba del Tiempo (improvised 2 hour bongo festival every Monday)
  • Soccer game (Super Classico between teams River and Boca - there will be at least one game each study abroad session)

    • Day/Weekend Trips
  • Highly recommended to travel in general
  • Travel to Brazil, Chile, or Uruguay - Fairly easy to arrange
  • Iguazu falls (right on the border of Brazil, national park with over 275 waterfalls, worth every second)
  • Mendoza (wine province in Argentina)
  • Bariloce (Ski resort during winter, Cool mountains during summer)
  • Cordoba (province where Oktoberfest is celebrated largely)
  • Puerto Madryn (city with lots of wildlife and beach life - whale watching, snorkeling, penguin colonies, etc.)
  • Salta and Jujuy (mountain of 14 colors)

    Last Minute Tips

    Currency
    If you run out of US Dollars, use Xoom.com. The rates are not as good as that of black market, but significantly better than official rate.There are distribution centers around the city. Bring your passport, fill paperwork, get money!
    Currency
    Bring US Dollars. Banks and airports have terrible currency exchange rates. Go to Casa de Cambio (informal, easy to find, and technically illegal shops) that give you a much better exchange rate (instead of 7 pesos/$, 12 pesos/$).
    Flight
    Don't book a group flight back from Buenos Aires after the program's over - You want some time to travel and explore.
    Host Family
    Understand that there will be problems living with anyone for 5 months. If the issues become too large, just switch host families.
